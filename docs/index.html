    • Changes in tariffs

    Trade has often being used as a whipping boy for loss of jobs and economic suffering, as displaced workers are unable to find new jobs and wind up sinking into economic depression. The following analysis has as its main objective to better understand the consequences of trade policies on the overall welfare of a country, more specifically comparing the economic effects faced by developed and developing countries.

    Overall Conclusion

    Although we could not find evidence that tariffs (and in turn, freer trade) had an effect on economic welfare in developed or developing countries, there remains an important message - Imposing tariffs do not improve welfare either. Politicians campaign on increased tariffs to protect jobs, a soothing message to their constituents. However, whilst tariffs may improve the lives of that small group whose jobs are protected, the larger population may not benefit as a result. Voters should be skeptical about pushes to increase tariffs, and instead ask, what can be done to ensure that people adversely affected by trade openness are compensated in one way or another.

    Tariffs

    Using yearly (1996 - 2016) data from the World Bank and World Integrated Trade Solution, we conducted an analysis of the relationship between tariff changes and the economy. The table below sets out the definition of the variables used:

    Dependent Variables Description
    GDP per Capita Growth Rate Year-on-year rate of change of GDP per capita
    Unemployment Rate Unemployed persons as percentage of total labor force
    Labor Force Participation Rate** Sum of employed and unemployed persons as a percentage of total population aged 15+
    Inflation Rate Year-on-year increase in consumer prices (%)

    Methodology

    As this is a panel dataset, we took first differences to compute the delta and percentage change. We used delta for GDP per Capita Growth rate changes, and percentage changes for the others, including tariff changes. As the impact of tariff changes are unlikely to be felt immediately, we compared T0 tariff changes to T+2 economic changes.
